Ladybird, a browser engine project led by Andreas Kling, has announced a strategic shift towards adopting Rust for its codebase, driven by the need for memory safety and enhanced security in systems programming. This move follows a previous exploration of Swift, which was abandoned due to poor C++ interoperability and limited cross-platform support outside Apple's ecosystem. Rust emerges as a pragmatic choice, given its mature ecosystem, strong safety guarantees, and growing adoption in major browsers like Firefox and Chromium. The decision reflects a broader industry trend towards memory-safe languages to mitigate vulnerabilities, with Ladybird aiming to gradually replace C++ components while maintaining compatibility. The technical foundation involves careful interop boundaries and a phased approach, balancing the benefits of Rust's ownership model with the existing C++ architecture's complexity.

The implementation focused on porting LibJS, Ladybird's JavaScript engine, which includes the lexer, parser, AST, and bytecode generatorâ€”totaling approximately 25,000 lines of Rust code. AI tools, specifically Claude Code and Codex, were employed for translation under human direction, involving hundreds of small prompts to steer the process. A critical requirement was ensuring byte-for-byte identical output between the C++ and Rust pipelines, enabling side-by-side diffs to catch bugs. This resulted in zero regressions across extensive test suites, including test262 (52,898 tests) and Ladybird's regression tests (12,461 tests), with no performance regressions. Andreas Kling emphasized that the generated Rust code intentionally mimics C++ patterns for compatibility, acknowledging it isn't idiomatic Rust but prioritizing correctness. The port was completed in about two weeks, a task estimated to take months manually, showcasing AI's efficiency in code migration.

The Age Verification Trap: Verifying age undermines everyone's data protection

The article examines the inherent conflict between age-verification mandates and data-privacy principles, framing it as an 'age-verification trap' where regulatory efforts to protect children online compel platforms to adopt intrusive data-collection practices. Technically, verification relies on two flawed approaches: identity-based methods like government ID uploads, which are often inaccessible or insecure, and inference-based systems using AI for facial age estimation or behavioral analysis, which introduce probabilistic errors and scalability issues. This creates a direct tension with modern data-protection laws such as GDPR, which emphasize data minimization, purpose limitation, and storage limitation, as platforms must log and retain evidence to prove compliance, effectively undermining privacy-by-design frameworks.

Specific implementations highlight these challenges: Meta deploys facial age estimation on Instagram via third-party partners, requiring selfie videos that trigger AI analysis and potential account locks; TikTok scans public videos for age inference; and Google uses behavioral signals tied to account activity, escalating to ID or credit card checks when uncertain. In regions like Brazil and Nigeria, weak identity infrastructure forces reliance on biometric inference and offshore vendors, increasing surveillance risks. The article notes that false positives and negatives are common, with appeals processes necessitating long-term data storage, and cites that 'platforms that collect less data start to look reckless by comparison' as enforcement pressures prioritize defensible systems over privacy-preserving ones.

This trend has profound societal implications, normalizing continuous surveillance and eroding internet anonymity, which could set a precedent for expanded data logging across digital services. Long-term, it risks chilling innovation in privacy engineering and shifting internet access toward identity-based models, fundamentally altering user freedom. The Hacker News community cares deeply because it involves critical trade-offs in system design, regulatory overreach, and the erosion of cryptographic trust models, sparking debates on technical solutions versus cultural shifts in child protection.

Strong enforcement of age rules undermines data privacy.
